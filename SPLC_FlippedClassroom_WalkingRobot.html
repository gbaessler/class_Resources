01

Where You Left Off

You have just completed your first prototype of a laser-cut walking robot — a plywood body powered by a hobby gearbox, with front and back legs driven by a crank-and-transmission mechanism. It was assembled from 3 mm plywood panels that slot together like LEGO pieces.

First-time prototypes are never perfect — and that is the point. Maybe your robot wobbles. Maybe the legs jam. Maybe the battery holder falls out during testing. Every one of these observations is valuable engineering data.

In this activity you will use the engineering design process to analyse what went wrong and plan a targeted improvement. Come to class ready to redesign, re-cut, and test.

Watch for… Why it matters for YOUR robot
Defining the problem clearly What is the specific, measurable problem with your robot? (wobble, leg jamming, battery falling out, slow speed?)
Criteria and constraints What must your robot do (criteria)? What can you NOT change — same motor, same materials (constraints)?
Brainstorming multiple ideas Engineers always generate several options before choosing one. How many ideas can you come up with for your problem?
Testing and measuring results How will you prove your improvement actually made things better? What will you measure?
Iterating — improve, don't scrap Good engineers tweak and refine. You are not starting from scratch; you are improving what you already built.
03

Reflect — Answer in Your Workbook

Write your answers to the following questions in your workbook or on paper. You will share and discuss them at the start of next class. Be honest and specific — vague answers won't help you in the workshop.

1
Observe Your Prototype

Describe one problem you noticed when you tested your walking robot. What exactly happened?

Example: "The back legs dragged on the ground instead of lifting, so the robot barely moved forward."
2
Root Cause Analysis

Using the design process from the video — what is the root cause of your problem? Design issue, material issue, or assembly issue?

Think about: leg geometry, joint friction, motor torque, weight distribution, crank offset…
3
Criteria & Constraints

Write two criteria (things your improved robot MUST do) and one constraint (a limit you cannot change).

Criteria: "must walk in a straight line for 30 cm."   Constraint: "must use the existing gearbox and 2× AA batteries."
4
Brainstorm Solutions

Sketch or list at least three different solutions to your problem. Quantity over quality at this stage — wild ideas welcome!

Longer legs? Different leg angle? Added weights? Rubber feet? Different crank position? All ideas count.
5
Select Your Best Idea

Which idea will you prototype next class, and why? Use at least one reason connected to the design process video.

Which directly addresses the root cause? Which can be tested quickly in one lesson?
6
Plan Your Test

How will you measure success next class? What test will you run, and what does "better" look like compared to your current prototype?

Be specific: "I'll measure distance walked in 10 seconds" beats "I'll see if it walks better."
04

Improvement Challenge Menu

Next class you will redesign parts in Tinkercad and re-cut them on the laser cutter. Choose at least one challenge to focus on. More demanding challenges earn additional design marks.

Challenge What to do Level
🦿 Leg Geometry The front leg is 80 mm, back leg 50 mm. The ratio and crank-pivot position directly affects stride length and walking efficiency. Redesign leg proportions to maximise forward movement per crank rotation. Entry
⚖️ Centre of Mass A robot that tips forward or sideways wastes energy. Analyse where your robot's weight sits and redesign body panels or battery placement so the centre of mass is directly over the foot contact points. Entry
🔩 Reduce Friction Wooden axle-and-lock joints create significant friction. Modify axle hole sizing, add wooden washers at each joint, or redesign the lock piece to reduce binding while keeping parts together. Intermediate
🐾 Foot Design Flat-ended legs slip on smooth surfaces. Redesign the foot ends of both leg types to improve grip — consider angled contact surfaces or curved shapes inspired by animal locomotion. Test on two different surfaces. Intermediate
🏗️ Alternating Gait Currently left and right legs move in parallel. Redesign the crank wheel so legs are 180° out of phase, creating an alternating walking gait. Requires modifying wheel geometry to carry two offset axle positions. Advanced
🔄 Steering Design a simple steering add-on — a passive caster under the body, or an asymmetric leg arrangement that creates a predictable curve. Document your design rationale with annotated sketches before cutting. Advanced

Which challenge did you choose? Write your selection and a one-sentence reason in your workbook. You'll pitch it to your group at the start of next class.

07

Extension Activities (Optional)

For students who want to extend their thinking beyond the core activity.

🤖 Extension A

Research Boston Dynamics Spot or Honda ASIMO. How do their leg geometries compare to your robot? How do they solve balance and friction? Write a five-point comparison in your workbook.

📋 Extension B

Engineers keep detailed lab notebooks. Photograph your current robot, annotate the image with the problems you found, then write a brief Engineering Change Order explaining each planned modification and why.

🔢 Extension C

The hobby gearbox reduces speed and multiplies torque. Research a typical yellow gearbox gear ratio. Calculate the output torque and RPM. Does your robot need more torque or more speed to walk better?
