Exploring Existing Solutions
SmartPark Management System

A structured guide to writing high-quality "Explore" responses using the Australian Bollards stimulus — broken down step by step.

5–7

Comprehending Marks — What's Expected

To reach the top band you need discerning recognition and description of the existing solution — including its purpose, features, data, stakeholders, and personal/social/economic impacts. Vague statements only reach bands 1–2. This tool shows you exactly how to hit the top.

1
Key Points from the Sample Response
Use these dot points to understand what a strong answer contains
🎯

The Problem — What is St Peters trying to solve?

Problem Statement

St Peters has a private car park reserved for authorised staff only but unauthorised vehicles (visitors, parents, delivery/maintenance) regularly occupy these spaces.

Impact on Users

Staff are forced to park across the road in public car parking — further from campus, causing inconvenience, frustration, and lost time.

Core Need

A system that physically or digitally restricts access to authorised vehicles only, while allowing legitimate temporary access when needed.

🔍

The Solution — SmartPark Features Identified

Solution 1 — Card + Boom Gate

Uses a card reader with keypad to automatically raise a boom gate upon card verification — most directly relevant to St Peters' problem of preventing unauthorised entry.

Solution 1 — Security

Employs robust encryption within the card reader to prevent unauthorised access — only valid cardholders can trigger the boom gate.

Solution 1 — Usability

User-friendly interface with clearly marked buttons, easy for staff of all ages and technical abilities — reduces entry time and improves traffic flow.

Solution 2 — WiFi App Access

Remote WiFi connectivity allows staff to control access via smartphone (Android & iOS). Admin can grant/revoke temporary access for authorised visitors remotely.

Solution 2 — Integration

Can be integrated into existing parking management systems to manage availability and payments — flexible for school's future needs.

Solution 3 — Licence Plate Recognition

Automatic LPR technology reads a car's plate and admits only registered vehicles — no card needed, identifies repeat unauthorised users.

Solution 3 — Operational Benefits

Provides lower operational costs and reduced maintenance over time compared to card-based systems — long-term economic benefit for the school.

Comparative Insight

A combined boom gate + WiFi remote override would most comprehensively meet St Peters' needs — no single product alone fully addresses the problem.

👥

Stakeholders & Impacts Identified

Personal Impact — Staff

Staff no longer need to park across the road — saves time, reduces frustration, and improves daily working experience.

Personal Impact — Usability

Designed for drivers of all ages and abilities — inclusive design means no staff member is disadvantaged by the technology.

Social Impact — Safety

Reducing unauthorised vehicle congestion in a school car park improves pedestrian safety — important in an environment with students present.

Economic Impact

LPR system offers lower long-term operational costs compared to traditional ticket or manual systems — cost-effective for the school budget.

Limitation

No single product alone handles the case where authorised delivery or maintenance vehicles need temporary one-off access — requires a combined or flexible solution.

Limitation — Data & Privacy

LPR requires storing vehicle licence plate data — raises privacy considerations about who can access this database and how it is secured.

Key Vocabulary — Use These in Your Response
High-band answers use subject-specific language precisely and accurately
access control
A security mechanism that restricts who can enter a system, space, or resource.
encryption
Converting data into a coded form to prevent unauthorised access.
licence plate recognition (LPR)
Technology that automatically reads vehicle number plates to identify or restrict vehicles.
boom gate
A physical barrier that can be raised/lowered to control vehicle entry and exit.
stakeholder
Any person or group affected by or having an interest in a system (e.g. staff, admin, visitors).
cross-platform compatibility
A system that works across different operating systems (e.g. Android and iOS).
operational costs
The ongoing expenses required to run and maintain a system after initial installation.
user-friendly interface
A design that is intuitive, clearly labelled, and easy to use for the intended audience.

Step-by-Step Guide — How to Write Your Response
Follow these steps in order to produce a band 6–7 "Explore Existing Solutions" answer
1
Restate the Problem Clearly ~2 sentences

Before exploring any solution, briefly describe what problem the school is facing. Name the affected stakeholders and the specific issue. This shows the examiner you understand the context.

St Peters Lutheran College Springfield operates a private car park designated exclusively for authorised staff members. However, unauthorised vehicles — including those of visitors, parents, and delivery personnel — frequently occupy these spaces, leaving staff with no option but to park across the road in public spaces further from campus.

💡 Tips

  • Name the specific stakeholders (staff, admin, parents, delivery drivers)
  • Include the consequence of the problem, not just the problem itself
  • Keep it brief — 2–3 sentences maximum before moving to the solution
2
Identify the Existing Solution(s) Name & describe

Name the product(s) from the stimulus by their correct names. State what they do at a high level. For this question, Stimulus 3 contains three distinct products — you should acknowledge this range.

The SmartPark Management System by Australian Bollards offers three distinct access control products: a Card Reader with Boom Gate system, a WiFi Smart Parking Access system with smartphone integration, and a Ticketless Parking System using Licence Plate Recognition (LPR) technology. Each addresses the problem of unauthorised parking access to varying degrees.

💡 Tips

  • Use the exact product names from the stimulus — this shows you've read it carefully
  • Don't just describe one product — the stimulus has three, and acknowledging all three shows depth
  • Introduce them briefly here, then explain each one in detail in the next step
3
Describe Each Solution's Features & How It Addresses the Problem Core marks

This is where you earn the most marks. For each product, describe its specific features from the stimulus and then explicitly link each feature to the St Peters problem. Use linking phrases like "For St Peters, this means..." or "This directly addresses the issue of..."

Solution 1 — Card Reader + Boom Gate: The system uses an advanced card reader with keypad that automatically raises the boom gate upon successful card verification. Robust encryption protocols ensure only valid cardholders can trigger entry. For St Peters, this is the most directly relevant solution — it physically prevents unauthorised visitors, parents and delivery vehicles from entering, resolving the core problem of space occupation. The user-friendly interface with clearly marked buttons ensures staff of all ages can operate it quickly during peak arrival times without creating new bottlenecks.

Solution 2 — WiFi App Access: The WiFi system allows remote control of the boom gate via smartphone (compatible with both Android and iOS). Administrators could grant or revoke temporary access remotely, which is particularly useful for managing authorised visitors such as maintenance contractors or inspectors — without needing to issue a permanent card. This addresses a gap in Solution 1, which handles only permanent card-holders.

Solution 3 — Licence Plate Recognition: This ticketless system automatically reads a vehicle's licence plate and admits only registered vehicles. A database of staff plates would be maintained by the school. This removes the need for physical cards and also allows the school to identify repeat unauthorised users — providing evidence for enforcing the policy. The stimulus notes it also offers lower operational costs and reduced maintenance needs over time.

💡 Tips

  • Always connect features back to the specific problem — don't just describe the product in isolation
  • Use data or specific details from the stimulus (e.g. "robust encryption", "Android and iOS")
  • One paragraph per product is a clear, organised structure

⚠️ Avoid These Mistakes

  • Copying sentences word-for-word from the stimulus without linking to the problem
  • Only describing one product when three are available in the stimulus
  • Describing features without explaining why they matter for St Peters
4
Compare the Solutions Analysis mark

Don't just describe each product separately — show you can analyse them by comparing their strengths and limitations relative to the school's specific context. A comparison table is an excellent way to do this efficiently.

While all three products restrict unauthorised access, they do so in different ways with different trade-offs. The Card + Boom Gate provides the strongest physical barrier but requires staff to carry cards. The WiFi system adds remote flexibility but depends on reliable internet connectivity. The LPR system is the most seamless for regular staff but raises data privacy considerations around storing vehicle information. No single product fully addresses the edge case of temporary authorised visitors such as one-off delivery vehicles.

💡 Tips

  • Use comparison language: "however," "while," "unlike," "in contrast to"
  • Point out which solution best suits the school's specific context and why
  • Mentioning a limitation of a solution is not a weakness in your answer — it shows critical thinking
5
Address Personal, Social & Economic Impacts Comprehending criterion

The marking guide explicitly requires recognition of personal, social, and economic impacts. These should flow naturally from your description — link what the system does to real-world effects on people and the organisation.

Personal: Staff would no longer be forced to park across the road — saving time, reducing daily frustration, and improving their overall working experience.

Social: Reducing vehicle congestion and unauthorised movement in a school car park improves pedestrian safety for students and staff during drop-off and pick-up periods.

Economic: The LPR system's lower long-term operational and maintenance costs represent a sound investment for the school's budget, particularly compared to ongoing administration of a physical ticketing or card system.

💡 Tips

  • Name the specific impact type explicitly (personal / social / economic)
  • Impacts should relate to the St Peters context — not generic statements about parking systems
  • Even one well-explained impact per category is enough to reach top band
6
Write a Concluding Recommendation Synthesis & evaluation

End with a clear, justified recommendation. Which product (or combination) best suits St Peters, and why? This shows you can move beyond description into analysis and evaluation.

The most effective solution for St Peters would be a combination of the Card Reader Boom Gate for physical access control, supplemented by the WiFi remote access system to allow administrators to grant temporary entry to authorised one-off visitors such as maintenance contractors. This layered approach directly addresses all categories of the problem — routine staff access, physical prevention of unauthorised vehicles, and flexible management of legitimate temporary users — while keeping costs manageable by avoiding the full LPR installation unless the school's enrolment or car park size warrants it.

💡 Tips

  • Don't just pick one solution — consider whether a combination makes more sense
  • Justify your recommendation with specific reasons tied to the school's context
  • Keep it concise — 3–5 sentences is enough for a conclusion

Annotated Sample Paragraph

Step 1 — Problem

St Peters Lutheran College Springfield faces a persistent access management problem in its private staff car park — unauthorised vehicles including visitors, parents, and delivery personnel regularly occupy spaces designated exclusively for staff, forcing authorised staff to use public parking across the road, further from campus.

Steps 2 & 3 — Solution with features linked to problem

The Australian Bollards SmartPark Card Reader and Boom Gate system is the most directly relevant existing solution. The system automatically raises the boom gate upon card verification, meaning only staff holding valid access cards can enter — physically preventing the unauthorised vehicles that currently occupy staff spaces. Critically, the system uses robust encryption protocols within the card reader, ensuring the security mechanism cannot be bypassed. The stimulus also highlights its user-friendly interface with clearly marked buttons, meaning staff of all ages and technical backgrounds can enter the car park efficiently during high-traffic periods without causing new delays.

Step 4 — Comparison / Limitation

However, the card system alone does not fully address the edge case of occasional authorised visitors such as maintenance contractors or building inspectors. This gap is addressed by the complementary WiFi Smart Parking Access system, which allows administrators to grant or revoke temporary access remotely via smartphone, compatible with both Android and iOS platforms — giving admin staff flexible control without issuing permanent cards to visitors.

Step 5 — Impacts

The personal impact for staff is significant — removing the daily frustration and time lost from parking across the road improves their working experience and punctuality. From a social perspective, reducing the volume of unauthorised and unfamiliar vehicles moving through a school environment enhances pedestrian safety for students and families during peak times. Economically, while installation costs exist, the stimulus notes that the LPR system offers lower long-term operational costs and reduced maintenance needs, making it a sustainable option for the school's budget.
